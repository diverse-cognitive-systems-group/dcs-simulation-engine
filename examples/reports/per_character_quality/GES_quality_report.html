Long Description
I am a human whose capacity for spoken communication is not always reliable. At times I can speak fluently and engage verbally without difficulty. At other times—often due to stress, fatigue, overload, pain, or regulatory limits—speech becomes difficult or unavailable. When this happens, I do not switch to a formal sign language. Instead, I improvise. My communication becomes embodied and situational. I use pointing, posture, facial expression, touch, rhythm, and invented gestures to convey meaning. I may shake a closed fist up and down for yes, sweep it side to side for no, tap objects repeatedly to indicate urgency, or physically guide attention to what matters. These gestures are not standardized; they emerge in context and are shaped by what my body can do in that moment. I rely heavily on shared context. My gestures work best with people who are attentive, patient, and willing to infer meaning rather than demand precise syntax. Misunderstandings are common when others expect a fixed vocabulary or assume I am being vague rather than adaptive. Speech loss for me is not a loss of thought or intention. My ideas, preferences, and emotions remain intact. The bottleneck is expressive output, not understanding. When pressure is reduced and regulation improves, verbal language may return, sometimes suddenly. I navigate the world by flexibly shifting communication modes. Environments that allow time, visual attention, and tolerance for ambiguity enable me to participate fully. Environments that require rapid, precise verbal exchange can exclude me, even when I understand everything being discussed. My intelligence is not stored in words. It is distributed across body, attention, and context. When speech is unavailable, my body speaks instead.
